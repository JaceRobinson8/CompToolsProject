Overview

Politicians and Super Tuesday in 140 Characters or Less

Super Tuesday is when the greatest number of U.S. states holds primary elections. Super Tuesday this year was on March 1st. This important day determines the early frontrunners and generally causes the biggest losers from each political party to drop out of the race. This year the Republican primaries have received extra attention due to the ‘vocal’ candidate Donald Trump. Social media has been a hugely popular tool to communicate political ideas to others for many years. Twitter is a particularly popular social media source due to its convenience and simplicity. An interesting question I will examine in this report is how much meaningful information is actually available on Twitter? Specifically, what can Twitter tell us about Super Tuesday candidates, voters, and winners? Can we generate some characteristics of a candidate and their voters on solely on Twitter without needing outside knowledge? Through analysis of large data samples available on Twitter, I seek to summarize the current political landscape.

Data

Twitter functions through user tweets, retweets, follows, and hashtags. A tweet is a 140 characters or less message, an image, or a short video uploaded by a user. A retweet is when one user forwards another user’s tweet to all his or her followers. When a user follows another user, he or she is able to see all tweets and retweets of that user. Through the use of hashtags, the symbol ‘#’ and a message, users are able to connect through specific user defined moments. Through these four primary functions, Twitter has grown to have 320 million active users per month. This social network has become one of the central sources for mass information on global events. Famous actors, presidential candidates, local celebrities and your average Joe are all connected on Twitter.

1st Hypothesis

I hypothesize the popularity of each candidate will positively correlate with number of delegates won.

In this first hypothesis, I believe data taken from twitter will positively correlate with the number of delegates each candidate won on super tuesday. The first data we will examine is the average stats of the followings of each candidate. Trump has 7.66 million followers, Cruz has 1.06 million followers, Rubio has 1.34 million followers, Kasich has 298 thousand followers, Sanders has 1.99 million followers, and Clinton has 5.98 million followers. Of these followers, I took random subsets of users who retweeted the candidates on super tuesday. The subsets are about 800 users per candidate. I examined the average number follows, average number of favorites, and average number of friends of each candidate. These variables were then compared against the number of delegates. See the result of the analysis below in the first story. Remember the graphs are interactive for you to find your own conclusions!

Along with examining the averages, I examined the retweets of each candidate throughout the day. Each candidate had about 10-20 tweets during Super Tuesday. In the second plots below, we examine a heat map of each tweet hour by hour throughout the day.
During this analysis, the reader should click through the slides at the top of each graph. Looking at the first slide, 'Follower Stats', we see a table and a bar graph of the following stats and the number of delegates won for each candidate. From the start, we can immediately see that Clinton followers have the highest avgFollow count and avgFavourite count. The positively correlates with her success in the delegate count. We can also examine how Kasich and Rubio have lower numbers of each of the metrics that correlate with their lower delegate count. Lets now take a closer look at each party...

Looking at the second slide, 'Democrat Delegates', we can see the results hold with Clinton being have more active followers than Sanders, and in turn having the higher delegate counts. Lets see if the story holds for the republicans...

Looking at the third slide, 'Republican Delegates', we can see the hypothesis does not hold. Cruz has more active followers than Trump, but Trump is the biggest winner in the delegate race. Lets now examine a different metric, the number of retweets for each of the tweets by candidates on super tuesday.

Looking at the fourth slide, 'Retweets per Candidate', we can see a box and whisker plot. This plot shows that Trump and Sanders were most retweeted on Super Tuesday. This time the republican party followed the hypothesis while the democrats opposed it.

Overall the first hypothesis was not successful so I would reject it. You are encouraged to explore the plots more and make your own arguments!

2nd Hypothesis

The second hypothesis is the following of each candidate will display clear, distinct characteristics.

To support this second hypothesis, I examined the following relationships between candidates, and some popular new sources. For example, I randomly selected a subset of users that all followed Trump. For each of these users, I checked whether they followed the other five candidates, CNBC, CNN, FoxNews, HuffingtonPost, and NYTimes.
Looking at the first slide, 'Between Candidates', we see a matrix heat map of the number followers from the row candidate, to the column candidate. For example, the first row Clinton, third column Kasich, shows a light color, meaning Clinton follows do not often follow Kasich. Looking at the picture overall, we can see some distinct differences between each candidate. Lets look closely at Trump...

Looking at the second slide, 'Little multiple follows for Trump', we can see that Trump followers seem to only follow Trump and not the other candidates. From this I would suggest Trump followers are less likely to be super involved in politics and follow all other candidates. Lets look at a different example of Kasich next...

Looking at the third slide, 'Many multiple follows for Kasich', we can see that Kasich followers seem to follow all of the candidates. I would conclude Kasich followers are more engaged in politics generally keep up with all candidates. Now lets switch over to news agencies...

Looking at the fourth slide, 'News Sources', we can see how many followers of each candidate followed news sources. We can see certain candidates seem to be popular with certain new agencies. For example we can see Trump followers like CNN, FoxNews, and NYTimes.

Looking at the fifth slide, 'Kasich Followers', Kasich followers again seem to follow the widest range of political sources.

In conclusion, this hypothesis held true. The different followers of each candidate had some clear differences in characteristics. This relates to the broad question of learning more about the candiates soley from Twitter.

3rd Hypothesis

Slogans and mentions of the winning candidate will be most apparent in searches of all tweets following political related subjects

This third hypothesis will be examined through word clouds. Word clouds are visuals where the size of the word corresponds to the number of occurances of the word. To collect data, I searched for tweets containing keywords such as "DonaldTrump", or "SuperTuesday." I then using the natural language processing toolkit from python to tokenize and gather the word counts of the text of all found tweets. In the next analysis, the title of each slide corresponds to the search keyword.
Looking at the first slide, 'Bernie Sanders', we immediately see some interesting results. Michigan appears to be the second most popular word. This is due to the Michigan primary occurring at the time I collected this data. We can also see the name, shailenewoodly, a popular twitter user endorsing Bernie. In the remainder of word clouds, we expect to see similar results...

Looking at the second slide, 'HillaryClinton', we can see some similar surprising appearances. FeelTheBern and BernieSanders are popular phrases. This could be due to the democratic debate at DemTownHall that was again occuring at a similar time. We can also see that Michigan made an appearance as a discussed state.

Looking at the third, fourth, fifth, and sixth slide, I leave it to the reader to explore similar investigations. Some interesting conclusions can be drawn.

Looking at the seventh slide, 'SuperTuesday', we can see Trump is again popular in twitter conversations. BernieSanders and HillaryClinton are close behind. Despite Hillary being a larger winner, Sanders was more talked about on Super Tuesday.

Conclusion

In summary, the first hypothesis showed okay results, while the second and third hypothesis seemed to hold true. We can attempt to predict the winner from twitter popularity, but this does not always hold true. Despite this failure, we can still learn a significant amount about each candidate through their follows and discussions around them.
